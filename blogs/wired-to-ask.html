Wired to Ask

2026-03-30

Written by Harrison Cook

Writing assisted by AI

Personal essay - not a research article

Somewhere along the way, I learned to pull threads. I learned not to stop at "it works," but to keep asking what lies underneath it. That compulsion has shaped everything about how I work: which problems I find important, what I can't let go of, and why some working relationships feel like a genuine meeting of minds while others feel like speaking entirely different languages.

Software has a uniquely deep disconnect compared to other industries. Usually, simply being human provides enough common ground to connect with people, but software is littered with incompatibilities I couldn't explain. I'd push on a decision and be told that a senior had made it, as if that closed the question. I realise now they were likely optimising for time or consensus, but to a brain that relies on foundational understanding, cutting off curiosity felt like leaving the job half-finished.

For a while, I tried to make the credential argument work. I assumed a shared degree meant shared wiring - that surviving the same curriculum produced similar neural pathways. But that's a loose proxy at best. Plenty of people get through rigorous programmes without being changed by them, and plenty without degrees have clearly survived an equivalent fire elsewhere.

So, what was this defining difference? In exploring the friction, I found a term from psychology that finally gave it shape: qualia - the subjective, first-person experience of thinking and doing. Two people can sit with the exact same problem and experience it completely differently. Having a word for that doesn't resolve the difference, but it makes it possible to talk about. This is my attempt to map my own qualia.

What Engineering Means to Me

The clearest place to start is with the word I use to describe what I am in this field. I have a BE Hons - an accredited engineering degree under the Washington Accord. That agreement exists specifically to ensure graduates have demonstrated foundational understanding, not just surface-level exposure. The expectation baked into my degree is the same one found in every traditional engineering discipline: understand the layer below your work. That is my bias. It's the lens I was trained through, and it colours everything that follows.

The term "software engineering" was born out of necessity at the 1968 NATO conference because projects were failing and the field needed the strict discipline the word implies. Early on, writing C enforced that discipline - you had to think about memory, hardware, and execution. Today, better tools and abstractions have lowered the barrier to entry, making development accessible to far more people, which is genuinely a good thing. But we all kept using the same word: engineer.

Today, the word means wildly different things depending on your context. The label debate itself doesn't interest me, but what the word asks of our education does. Most software roles today are closer to an electrician than an electrical engineer, and that's perfectly fine - the industry needs far more of the former. But the need for electrical engineers didn't disappear just because electricians outnumber them. Some problems still require that depth.

I've wrestled with the "sunk cost" argument, wondering if this kind of deep engineering is genuinely becoming redundant in software. My instinct is that it isn't. Depth doesn't disappear just because the abstraction moves up. Take React: on the surface, it's a UI library, but underneath there's a reconciler, a virtual DOM, a rendering pipeline, and if you're using SSR, an entire server-side execution model. Someone who traces those foundations isn't just a frontend developer; they're a software engineer who happens to work in the browser. People experiencing the same tool differently is a matter of qualia, not a matter of who is right or wrong.

How I Think

My memory doesn't retain things I'm not actively using. If I context-switch into a part of the stack I haven't touched in a month, it's mostly gone. Fullstack work on something like AWS is a particular kind of nightmare for this - there's just too much surface area to keep warm. Because of this real limitation, I have to think from the ground up.

When I hold the underlying ideas, rules and best practices become logical consequences rather than trivia I have to memorise. A pointer is a memory address; contiguous allocation is elements side-by-side. An array is those two ideas composed. A hash map is an array plus a hashing function. None of it is magic - just well-layered ideas, all the way down. For me, this ability to derive is load-bearing; it's what I have instead of recall.

I know there are people whose qualia is almost the exact opposite. They can hold an enormous amount of context warm, recall exact methods without pausing, and navigate broad surface areas without losing the thread. Teams absolutely need both. The problem isn't that this difference exists; the problem is not knowing it's there.

Much of this crystallised for me through years of feeling like I was doing C# "wrong" - not incorrectly, just wrong for me. Two senior C# developers can write code that reads like entirely different languages because the coherence lives in the programmer, not the tool. Ultimately, different stylistic approaches compile down to similar IL, and the JIT optimises from there. The disagreements I had were never about performance; they were about whose qualia the community had decided to optimise for. I don't want a language that reads like an English sentence - I want one that reads like intent.

Because my compulsion to understand everything from the ground up is already cognitively expensive, a tool that demands I also manage its structure compounds that cost for no reason. This is why Go works well for me. It has strong opinions about formatting, error handling, and package structure. I don't agree with all of it, but I don't need to - learning it once is a far smaller ask than constantly navigating a language that leaves all structural negotiation up to me.

Where This Leaves Me

I keep a line in my dotfiles that I come back to a lot:

Take care of the ideas, and the implementations take care of themselves.

Ideas don't change with context; solutions do. That is the core of how I think about engineering, and it's been the most reliable compass I've found in this industry.

Writing this post has been as much about understanding my own qualia as it has been about understanding others'. I'm not particularly interested in labels - senior, junior, frontend, backend. What I care about is how someone experiences the work. I want to find people whose qualia is visible to them. I want to work with people who can engage with a problem from their own foundation, tell me why something is wrong rather than just that it feels wrong, and recognise when our mental models diverge instead of assuming one of us is just mistaken. That's the kind of collaboration that actually moves things forward.

If you've ever felt like the standard way of doing things in this industry doesn't quite fit how your brain works, I genuinely want to hear from you. Not because I think my way is right, but because the most interesting problems I've found in this field have come from people who experience it differently than I do.
