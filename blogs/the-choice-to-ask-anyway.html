Wired to Ask

2026-03-30

Ideas and approval by Harrison Cook, writing assisted by AI

I've been thinking a lot lately about what engineering actually means - not the job title, but the discipline. The word gets used so loosely in software that I think it's lost some of its original weight. But the longer I sit with it, the more I think the real issue isn't the word - it's qualia: the differences in how people subjectively experience thinking, problem-solving, and the work itself. This is my attempt to map my own, understand how it differs from others around me, and articulate why I think that distinction actually matters.

What Engineering Means to Me

Every traditional engineering discipline carries an implicit expectation: understand the layer below your work. A structural engineer understands material science. An electrical engineer understands circuit theory. In some places "engineer" is even a licensed title - Canada, parts of Europe, Brazil. Software has largely developed without that weight, and Casey Muratori and DHH have both written about what that costs us.

What it has cost me, specifically, is time - spent identifying problems that others on the same team couldn't see, fighting fires that felt invisible to everyone else, and struggling to explain why something was wrong to people whose experience of the codebase was fundamentally different from mine. And it's gone the other way too: people have spent real effort trying to teach me things that never clicked, not because the lessons were wrong, but because the way they experienced the problem didn't map to the way I did. That's not a failing on anyone's part. It's what happens when people with different qualia are handed the same job title and expected to share the same mental model of the work.

Low-level development used to enforce this - if you were close enough to the hardware, it demanded understanding. That's no longer the default. Most software is written far enough up the stack that you can operate indefinitely on assumptions you've never tested, patterns you've never traced back to their origin. Without the curiosity to go look anyway, those assumptions quietly become the foundation of your practice - and some of them are just wrong. Not wrong in a way that breaks anything immediately, but wrong in a way that makes the system harder to reason about over time. The thing is, if everyone on a team shares the same assumptions, the same gaps, the same mental model - it works. The incoherence is invisible because it's consistent. The problems only surface when someone wired differently shows up.

This isn't an argument for low-level development. The curiosity to understand what's underneath is available at any layer. Take React - on the surface it's a UI library, but underneath there's a reconciler, a virtual DOM, a rendering pipeline, and if you're using SSR, a whole server-side execution model. Someone who has traced those foundations isn't just a frontend developer - they're a software engineer who happens to work in the browser. The layer doesn't matter. The neural wiring does.

Deriving vs Memorising

My memory doesn't retain things I'm not actively using. Context-switch into a part of the stack I haven't touched in a month and it's mostly gone - the specific flags, the service quirks, the config that only makes sense if you've been staring at it all week. Fullstack work on something like AWS is a particular kind of nightmare for this; there's just too much surface area to keep warm. It's a real limitation, and I don't pretend otherwise.

So I think from the ground up instead. When I hold the underlying ideas, rules and best practices become logical consequences rather than trivia to recall. I don't need a rule for every edge case because the core concepts cover the ground for me. A pointer is just a memory address referring to another location. Contiguous allocation is just elements stored side-by-side for efficient hardware access. An array is those two ideas composed together. A hash map is an array plus a hashing function. None of it is magic - it's just well-layered ideas, all the way down.

We wrap these compositions in labels - patterns, paradigms, architectures - and those labels are genuinely useful for communication. But they aren't understanding. Understanding is the ability to tear the label off and see what's underneath. For me, that ability is load-bearing - it's what I have instead of recall. I can adapt when the problem doesn't fit a familiar shape because I'm not relying on the shape.

There are people whose qualia is almost the opposite of mine - who can hold an enormous amount of context warm, recall the exact method for a situation without pausing, navigate a broad surface area without losing the thread. That's a genuinely different and valuable way of experiencing the work. Teams need both. The problem isn't the difference - it's not knowing it's there.

What the Industry Taught Me

The industry can't function if every decision gets thought through from first principles. You have to move forward, often with incomplete understanding, and that's okay. Shipping matters. Deadlines matter. Making money matters. None of those things are the enemy of engineering - they're just variables. But like all variables, they're constantly updating and need to be re-evaluated.

This is where qualia starts to compound the problem. When a team shares the same gaps - the same untested assumptions, the same patterns nobody has traced back - the process calcifies around those gaps. Retrospectives happen, but they don't surface what nobody can see. Legacy systems accumulate decisions that made sense to the people who made them, and new people inherit those decisions as gospel without inheriting the mental model behind them. Eventually nobody remembers why it exists - they just know it's "how things are done." The qualia that built the system is gone, and what's left looks like tradition.

What C# Taught Me

A lot of where my ideas came from was my own experience - and a big part of that experience was wrestling with C#.

C# offers a dozen ways to solve any given problem, but rarely has an opinion on which one you should pick. For someone trying to reason from first principles, that lack of direction is exhausting. I spent a long time looking for the "right way" to write C# and kept hitting walls. Two senior C# developers can write code that looks like entirely different languages, because the coherence lives in the programmer, not the tool.

What makes this even more frustrating is that the compiler and JIT are good enough to optimise most of these stylistic differences away. Different approaches frequently compile down to very similar IL, and the JIT optimises further from there. So the "rules" about how to write C# aren't really about performance - they're about what the community has decided is the most human-friendly way to express things. And the human they designed for isn't me.

C# is commonly praised for being readable because it resembles English. But English is full of ambiguity - the same sentence can mean entirely different things depending on context, tone, and assumption. Modelling a programming language after that has given me more headaches than I can count. I don't want a language that reads like prose. I want one that reads like intent.

None of this is to say C# is a bad language - it clearly works brilliantly for a lot of people, and I respect that. But for me it's a genuine hindrance. I've met other engineers who share that struggle without quite being able to articulate why. My current hypothesis is that it comes down to qualia again - the language optimises for one way of experiencing code, and if your internal experience doesn't match, you're fighting the tool instead of using it.

What I Actually Need from a Tool

This is where qualia becomes practical. The struggle with C# taught me something concrete about what I need - not what's objectively better, but what works for the way I process things. I need sensible defaults and strong opinions. When a tool has opinions, I can quickly determine whether it's suitable for my existing problem space. If it is, I spend less time deciding how to express something and more time on what I'm expressing. The difference between fighting a tool and flowing with it is night and day.

Go is a good example of this working well for me. The language has strong opinions about formatting, error handling, and package structure. I don't agree with all of them, but I don't need to - the fact that they exist means I can evaluate them once and move on. The cognitive load drops dramatically when the tool handles the structure and lets me focus on the ideas.

Where This Leaves Me

I keep a line in my dotfiles that I come back to a lot:

Take care of the ideas, and the implementations take care of themselves.

Ideas don't change with context. Solutions do. When the ideas are sound, the implementations follow. That's the core of how I think about engineering, and it's been the most reliable compass I've found in this industry.

Writing this post has been as much about understanding my own qualia as it has been about understanding others'. I'm not particularly interested in labels - senior, junior, frontend, backend. What I care about is how someone experiences the work. I want to find people whose qualia is visible to them - who can engage with a problem from their own foundation, tell me why something is wrong rather than just that it feels wrong, and recognise when our mental models diverge instead of assuming one of us is just mistaken. That's the kind of collaboration that actually moves things forward.

If you've ever felt like the standard way of doing things in this industry doesn't quite fit how your brain works - I'd genuinely like to hear from you. Not because I think my way is right, but because the most interesting problems I've found in this field have come from people who experience it differently than I do.
