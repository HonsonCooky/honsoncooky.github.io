Wired to Ask

2026-03-30

Ideas and approval by Harrison Cook, writing assisted by AI

I've been thinking a lot lately about what engineering actually means - not the job title, but the discipline. The word gets used so loosely in software that I think it's lost some of its original weight. This is my attempt to articulate where my head is at in 2026 - the understanding I'm currently operating under, where it came from, and why I think it matters.

What Engineering Means to Me

Engineering, at its root, is the application of understanding to solve practical problems. A structural engineer understands material science. An electrical engineer understands circuit theory. Across every traditional engineering discipline, the expectation is that you understand the layer below your work - not because it's interesting, but because the work demands it.

I think software engineering should carry the same expectation. And I think the reason it often doesn't is that software gives you the option. Write firmware and the hardware will demand you understand it. Write high-level application code and the abstractions are good enough that you can ship indefinitely without ever asking what's underneath. The engineering mindset, to me, is the neural wiring that makes you ask anyway.

That's available at any level of the stack. Take React - on the surface it's a UI library, but underneath it there's a reconciler, a virtual DOM, a rendering pipeline, and if you're using SSR, a whole server-side execution model to reason about. Someone who understands those foundations isn't just a frontend developer - they're a software engineer who happens to work in the browser. It's never about the layer. It's about whether that curiosity to understand what's underneath is in you.

Deriving vs Memorising

My memory doesn't retain things I'm not actively using. Context-switch into a part of the stack I haven't touched in a month and it's mostly gone - the specific flags, the service quirks, the config that only makes sense if you've been staring at it all week. Fullstack work on something like AWS is a particular kind of nightmare for this; there's just too much surface area to keep warm. It's a real limitation, and I don't pretend otherwise.

So I think from the ground up instead. When you hold the underlying ideas, rules and best practices become logical consequences rather than trivia to recall. You don't need a rule for every edge case because the core concepts cover the ground for you. A pointer is just a memory address referring to another location. Contiguous allocation is just elements stored side-by-side for efficient hardware access. An array is those two ideas composed together. A hash map is an array plus a hashing function. None of it is magic - it's just well-layered ideas, all the way down.

We wrap these compositions in labels - patterns, paradigms, architectures - and those labels are genuinely useful for communication. But they aren't understanding. Understanding is the ability to tear the label off and see what's underneath. When you can do that, the labels become natural rather than memorised, and you can adapt when the problem doesn't fit a familiar shape.

Not everyone thinks this way, and that's genuinely a good thing. There are people who can hold an enormous amount of context in their heads - who know the AWS service matrix cold, or can recall the exact LINQ method for a given situation without pausing. That's a real talent, and teams need it. The trick is knowing which kind of brain you have and putting it where it actually belongs.

What the Industry Taught Me

The industry can't function if every decision gets thought through from first principles. You have to move forward, often with incomplete understanding, and that's okay. Shipping matters. Deadlines matter. Making money matters. None of those things are the enemy of engineering - they're just variables. But like all variables, they're constantly updating and need to be re-evaluated.

The industry has at least started to acknowledge this - iterative development, retrospectives, the idea that nothing ships fully formed. But that attitude tends to stop at the code. Legacy systems are full of decisions that made sense once and have been silently load-bearing ever since. Conditions change, assumptions expire, and what worked yesterday might not work tomorrow. Treating any constraint as fixed is where things start to go wrong.

I think a lot of frustration in engineering teams comes from this. People build processes and best practices that make sense at the time, and then never revisit them. The process calcifies. New people inherit it as gospel. And eventually nobody remembers why it exists in the first place - they just know it's "how things are done." That's not engineering. That's tradition.

What C# Taught Me

A lot of where my ideas came from was my own experience - and a big part of that experience was wrestling with C#.

C# offers a dozen ways to solve any given problem, but rarely has an opinion on which one you should pick. For someone trying to reason from first principles, that lack of direction is exhausting. I spent a long time looking for the "right way" to write C# and kept hitting walls. Two senior C# developers can write code that looks like entirely different languages, because the coherence lives in the programmer, not the tool.

What makes this even more frustrating is that the compiler and JIT are good enough to optimise most of these stylistic differences away. Different approaches frequently compile down to very similar IL, and the JIT optimises further from there. So the "rules" about how to write C# aren't really about performance - they're about what the community has decided is the most human-friendly way to express things. And the human they designed for isn't me.

C# is commonly praised for being readable because it resembles English. But English is full of ambiguity - the same sentence can mean entirely different things depending on context, tone, and assumption. Modelling a programming language after that has given me more headaches than I can count. I don't want a language that reads like prose. I want one that reads like intent.

None of this is to say C# is a bad language - it clearly works brilliantly for a lot of people, and I respect that. But for me it's a genuine hindrance. I've met other engineers who share that struggle without quite being able to articulate why, and my current hypothesis is that this might be a big part of it: the language optimises for a kind of readability that actively works against how some of us think.

What I Actually Need from a Tool

That struggle taught me something concrete about what I need. I need sensible defaults and strong opinions. When a tool has opinions, I can quickly determine whether it's suitable for my existing problem space. If it is, I spend less time deciding how to express something and more time on what I'm expressing. The difference between fighting a tool and flowing with it is night and day.

Go is a good example of this working well for me. The language has strong opinions about formatting, error handling, and package structure. I don't agree with all of them, but I don't need to - the fact that they exist means I can evaluate them once and move on. The cognitive load drops dramatically when the tool handles the structure and lets me focus on the ideas.

Where This Leaves Me

I keep a line in my dotfiles that I come back to a lot:

Take care of the ideas, and the implementations take care of themselves.

Ideas don't change with context. Solutions do. When the ideas are sound, the implementations follow. That's the core of how I think about engineering, and it's been the most reliable compass I've found in this industry.

I'm not claiming any of this is right for everyone. It's shaped by my own experience, my own biases, and the handful of people I've been lucky enough to learn from. But it's been right for me, and if you've ever felt like the standard way of doing things in this industry doesn't quite fit how your brain works - you're probably not alone.
