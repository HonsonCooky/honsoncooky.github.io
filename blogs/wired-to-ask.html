Wired to Ask

2026-03-30

Written by Harrison Cook

Writing assisted by AI

Somewhere along the way I learned to pull threads - to not stop at what works, but keep asking what's underneath it. That learned behaviour has shaped everything about how I work: which problems I find important and which I find trivial, what I can't leave alone, and why some working relationships have felt like a genuine meeting of minds while others felt like speaking different languages entirely.

No other industry I've worked in has had this level of disconnect. In most contexts, being human has been enough of a common foundation - I can find the logical way to operate alongside people even when we're very different. But software has been littered with incompatibilities I couldn't explain. I'd push on a decision and be told that a senior had made it, as if that closed the question. I've worked in other industries where seniority genuinely mattered - but even there, it was mostly a surface thing, a respect and experience gradient. It was never a substitute for reasoning. Software is an intellectual playground. The idea that curiosity should stop at a pay grade made no sense to me. For a while I tried to make the credential argument work - a shared degree is a reasonable shorthand, having survived the same curriculum tends to produce similar neural pathways. But it's a loose proxy at best. Plenty of people get through a rigorous programme without really being changed by it, and plenty of people without one have clearly been through an equivalent fire somewhere else. The credential was never the thing itself. So what was this thing - this compulsion to understand rather than just to use - and why did it feel like the defining difference in so many of my working relationships?

In exploring that friction, I came across a term from psychology that finally gave it shape: qualia - the subjective, first-person experience of thinking and doing. Two people can sit with the same problem and experience it completely differently. Having a word for that doesn't resolve the difference, but it does make it possible to talk about - and that's where understanding can actually start. This is my attempt to map my own qualia.

What Engineering Means to Me

The clearest place I can start is with the word I use to describe what I do. I have a BE Hons - an accredited engineering degree under the Washington Accord. That agreement exists specifically to ensure graduates have demonstrated foundational understanding, not just exposure. The expectation baked into that degree is the same one you'll find across every traditional engineering discipline: understand the layer below your work. A structural engineer understands material science. An electrical engineer understands circuit theory. That's my bias. It's the lens I was trained through and it colours how I see everything that follows.

Software started as programming - the term "software engineering" didn't exist until the complexity demanded it, formally introduced at the 1968 NATO conference because projects were failing and the field needed the discipline the word implies. Early on, the work itself enforced that discipline - writing C meant thinking about memory, hardware, and execution. As the industry matured and better tools emerged, the barrier to entry dropped. Software writing and development became accessible to far more people, which is genuinely a good thing. But we all kept using the same word: engineer.

What that word means now varies wildly depending on where you are. In some cultures it's a credential backed by accredited education. In others it's a corporate title on an offer letter, unrelated to any specific training. The blurriness is something Casey Muratori and DHH have both written about, and I've tried discussing it online too - it goes nowhere. People defend what the word means in their circle, and what any particular team calls their roles is semantics. I don't care about that. What I care about is what the word asks of education. My BE Hons was built on a specific expectation: foundational understanding, not just exposure - that's the whole point of the Washington Accord accreditation. My worry - and I want to be clear this is a worry, not a claim - is that if the word drifts far enough in industry, the degree eventually drifts with it. There's a feedback loop there that seems plausible to me: industry signals what it wants, education adjusts to reflect that, and what gets quietly deprioritised is exactly the part that made the engineering degree distinct. I don't have data on this. But having been through a curriculum that went much deeper than most of the roles I've encountered since, I'd hate to see what was most valuable about that training slowly optimised away.

Before anyone takes this as a call to write more assembly - it isn't. The compulsion to understand will show up everywhere. Take React - on the surface it's a UI library, but underneath there's a reconciler, a virtual DOM, a rendering pipeline, and if you're using SSR, a whole server-side execution model. Someone who has traced those foundations isn't just a frontend developer - they're a software engineer who happens to work in the browser. You'll find plenty of criticism of React online - people who can't get comfortable with the abstraction and look for justifications in the framework itself. Someone else comes along and says it works fine for them. Neither is wrong. They're just experiencing it differently, and without that language, the whole thing dissolves into semantics. What level of the stack you work at doesn't determine your qualia.

How I Think

My memory doesn't retain things I'm not actively using. Context-switch into a part of the stack I haven't touched in a month and it's mostly gone. Fullstack work on something like AWS is a particular kind of nightmare for this - there's just too much surface area to keep warm. It's a real limitation, and I don't pretend otherwise. So I think from the ground up instead. When I hold the underlying ideas, rules and best practices become logical consequences rather than trivia to recall. A pointer is a memory address. Contiguous allocation is elements side-by-side. An array is those two ideas composed. A hash map is an array plus a hashing function. None of it is magic - just well-layered ideas, all the way down. For me, that ability to derive is load-bearing - it's what I have instead of recall.

There are people whose qualia is almost the opposite - who can hold an enormous amount of context warm, recall the exact method for a situation without pausing, navigate a broad surface area without losing the thread. That's a genuinely different and valuable way of experiencing the work. Teams need both. The problem isn't the difference - it's not knowing it's there.

Much of this crystallised through years of feeling like I was doing C# wrong - not incorrectly, just wrong for me. Two senior C# developers can write code that reads like entirely different languages, because the coherence lives in the programmer, not the tool. What eventually gave me stability was understanding that the compiler and JIT largely erase the stylistic differences anyway - different approaches compile down to similar IL, and the JIT optimises from there. The disagreements were never about performance. They were about whose qualia the community had decided to optimise for. C# and SQL are both praised for resembling English. But I don't want a language that reads like a sentence - I want one that reads like intent.

The compulsion to understand is already expensive. A tool that demands I also manage its structure compounds that cost for no reason. Go works well for me because it has strong opinions about formatting, error handling, and package structure - including folder layout, which maps directly to package boundaries. I don't agree with all of it, but I don't need to. Learning it once was a far smaller ask than navigating a language that left all of that to me.

Where This Leaves Me

I keep a line in my dotfiles that I come back to a lot:

Take care of the ideas, and the implementations take care of themselves.

Ideas don't change with context. Solutions do. When the ideas are sound, the implementations follow. That's the core of how I think about engineering, and it's been the most reliable compass I've found in this industry.

Writing this post has been as much about understanding my own qualia as it has been about understanding others'. I'm not particularly interested in labels - senior, junior, frontend, backend. What I care about is how someone experiences the work. I want to find people whose qualia is visible to them - who can engage with a problem from their own foundation, tell me why something is wrong rather than just that it feels wrong, and recognise when our mental models diverge instead of assuming one of us is just mistaken. That's the kind of collaboration that actually moves things forward.

If you've ever felt like the standard way of doing things in this industry doesn't quite fit how your brain works - I'd genuinely like to hear from you. Not because I think my way is right, but because the most interesting problems I've found in this field have come from people who experience it differently than I do.
