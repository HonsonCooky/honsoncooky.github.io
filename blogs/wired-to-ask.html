Wired to Ask

2026-03-30

Written by Harrison Cook

Personal essay - not a research article

Abstract - Despite sharing the title "software engineer," practitioners in this industry frequently experience a systemic, hidden friction in collaboration. This disconnect rarely stems from the code itself, but rather from fundamentally incompatible cognitive approaches to problem-solving and system design. This essay treats that interpersonal friction as a bug. By applying the philosophical concept of qualia - the subjective, first-person experience of thinking - it explores why appealing to seniority or shared credentials fails to bridge the gap. Ultimately, this piece maps the specific qualia of deriving systems from first principles, examines what the title "engineer" demands of our technical education, and proposes that understanding how we think is the key to resolving collaborative incompatibilities.

The Friction

I used to think my relentless curiosity was just a personality trait. But over time, I've come to realise it's a learned behaviour. When your early experiences teach you that the surface isn't always stable, you learn to look underneath. You pull threads. Understanding exactly why something works isn't just an intellectual exercise; it's a mechanism for building trust. I learned early on that I couldn't simply accept that a system functioned - I had to see the foundations to know it wouldn't collapse.

That compulsion to not stop at "it works," but to keep digging until I hit bedrock, has shaped everything about how I operate. It dictates which problems I find important, what I find impossible to let go of, and why some working relationships have felt like a genuine meeting of minds while others felt like speaking different languages entirely.

Software has a uniquely deep disconnect compared to other industries. Usually, simply being human provides enough common ground to connect with people (if you try), but software is littered with incompatibilities I couldn't explain. I'd push on a technical decision and be told that a senior had made it, as if that closed the question. I realise now they were likely optimising for time or consensus, but in a discipline built entirely on logic and derived truths, cutting off inquiry at a pay grade felt like leaving the job half-finished.

I treated this friction the same way I treat a bug: I wanted to understand the underlying mechanism. Having come from a formal educational institution, an easy trap to fall into was the credential argument. It's tempting to assume that a shared degree means shared wiring. But that hypothesis never really held water for me. A credential is a loose proxy at best. Plenty of people get through rigorous programmes without fundamentally changing how they think, and plenty of people without degrees have clearly forged that same analytical depth elsewhere.

If the credential wasn't the root cause, what was? In searching for a better mental model, I found a term from the philosophy of mind that finally gave it shape: qualia - the subjective, first-person experience of thinking and doing. Two people can sit with the exact same problem and experience it completely differently. Having a precise word for that doesn't resolve the difference, but it provides the framework to actually talk about it. This is my attempt to map my own qualia.

What Engineering Means to Me

The clearest place to start is with the word I use to describe what I am in this field. I have a BE Hons - an accredited engineering degree recognised under the Washington Accord. While that agreement exists primarily for the mutual international recognition of qualifications, the baseline it enforces is that graduates must demonstrate foundational understanding, not just surface-level exposure. The expectation baked into my degree is the same one found in every traditional engineering discipline: understand the layer below your work. That is my baseline. It's the lens I was trained through, and it colours everything that follows.

The term "software engineering" was formalised out of necessity at the 1968 NATO conference because projects were failing and the field needed the strict discipline the word implies. Early on, writing C enforced that discipline - you had to think about memory, hardware, and execution. Today, better tools and abstractions have lowered the barrier to entry, making development accessible to far more people, which is genuinely a good thing. But we all kept using the same word: engineer.

Today, the word means wildly different things depending on your context. The label debate itself doesn't interest me, but what the word asks of our technical education does. Most software roles today are closer to an electrician than an electrical engineer, and that's perfectly fine - the industry needs far more of the former. But the need for electrical engineers didn't disappear just because electricians outnumber them. Some problems still require that structural depth.

I've wrestled with the "sunk cost" argument, wondering if this kind of deep engineering is genuinely becoming redundant in modern software. My instinct is that it isn't. Depth doesn't disappear just because the abstraction moves up. Take React: on the surface, it's a UI library, but underneath there's a reconciler, a virtual DOM, a rendering pipeline, and if you're using SSR, an entire server-side execution model. Someone who traces those foundations isn't just a frontend developer; they're a software engineer who happens to work in the browser. People experiencing the same tool differently is a matter of differing qualia, not a matter of who is right or wrong.

How I Think

My memory doesn't retain things I'm not actively using. If I context-switch into a part of the stack I haven't touched in a month, the specific syntax is mostly gone. Fullstack work on something like AWS is a particular kind of nightmare for this - there's just too much surface area to keep warm. Because of this real cognitive limitation, I've had to engineer a different approach: thinking from the ground up.

When I hold the underlying ideas, rules and best practices become logical consequences rather than trivia I have to memorise. A pointer is a memory address; contiguous allocation is elements side-by-side. An array is those two ideas composed. A hash map is an array plus a hashing function. None of it is magic - just well-layered ideas, all the way down. For me, this ability to derive is load-bearing; it's the system I use instead of recall.

I know there are people whose qualia is almost the exact opposite. They can hold an enormous amount of context warm, recall exact methods without pausing, and navigate broad surface areas without losing the thread. Teams absolutely need both. The problem isn't that this difference exists; the problem is not knowing it's there.

Much of this crystallised for me through years of feeling like I was doing C# "wrong" - not incorrectly, just wrong for me. Two senior C# developers can write code that reads like entirely different languages because the coherence lives in the programmer, not the tool. Ultimately, different stylistic approaches compile down to similar IL, and the JIT optimises from there. The friction I felt was never about performance; it was about whose qualia the community had decided to optimise for. I don't want a language that reads like an English sentence - I want one that reads like intent.

Because my compulsion to derive everything from first principles is already cognitively expensive, a tool that demands I also manage its structure compounds that cost for no measurable return. This is why Go works well for me. It has strict, systemic opinions about formatting, error handling, and package structure. I don't agree with all of it, but I don't need to - learning its constraints once is a far smaller ask than constantly navigating a language that leaves all structural negotiation up to me.

Where This Leaves Me

I keep a line in my dotfiles that I come back to a lot:

Take care of the ideas, and the implementations take care of themselves.

Ideas don't change with context; solutions do. That is the core of how I think about engineering, and it's been the most reliable compass I've found in this industry.

Writing this post has been as much about rigorously defining my own qualia as it has been about understanding others'. I'm not particularly interested in labels - senior, junior, frontend, backend. What I care about is how someone experiences the work. I want to find people whose qualia is visible to them. I want to work with people who can engage with a problem from their own foundation, tell me why a system is flawed rather than just that it feels wrong, and recognise when our mental models diverge instead of assuming one of us is just mistaken. That's the kind of analytical collaboration that actually moves things forward.

If you've ever felt like the standard way of doing things in this industry doesn't quite fit how your brain works, I genuinely want to hear from you. Not because I think my way is the only right way, but because the most interesting solutions I've found in this field have come from people who experience the problem differently than I do.
